How does matplotlib decides the bins? Actually matplotlib's hist function uses numpy's histogram function under the hood.

  • matplotlib.pyplot.hist() calls numpy.histogram() internally to calculate the bins for the histogram.
  • If numpy.histogram() is used without specifying the number of bins, it determines the optimal number of bins based on the data distribution. However, if we use matplotlib.pyplot.hist() without specifying the bins parameter, it defaults to 10 bins.
  • We can control the bin selection using options like 'auto', 'sturges', 'fd', etc., which yield different outcomes depending on the dataset size and characteristics.
    • The 'auto' strategy selects a suitable number of bins based on the data distribution and may use other rules such as 'sturges' or 'fd' depending on the data characteristics.
    • If bins is omitted in matplotlib.pyplot.hist(), it will use a default of 10 bins.

Do you see the issues with having too few bins or too many bins? In particular, do you notice weird patterns that emerge from bins=30?

  • When the number of bins is too small, the histogram becomes overly simplified. It cannot accurately reflect the distribution's detailed characteristics. For example, bins=2 or bins=3 only capture the most basic trend in the data, which might mask important details like peaks or irregularities.
  • With a very high number of bins, the histogram becomes too detailed, and the bars represent very narrow ranges. The result can be noisy, with many individual spikes that may not represent meaningful patterns but instead random variations in the data.
  • When using bins=30, I can notice patterns such as small peaks and troughs that appear to fluctuate. These patterns could be artifacts caused by the interaction between the exact data points and the bin boundaries. Sometimes, the choice of bin size can emphasize certain minor variations or outliers, making them appear more important than they really are.

Why do you see such patterns?

The patterns you observe in the histogram with bins=60 arise due to the interaction between the discrete nature of the IMDB_Rating data and the chosen bin size. Ratings are typically given in whole numbers or increments like 0.5, leading to clusters at these specific values. When the bin size does not align well with these common rating points, it creates alternating peaks and empty bars. Peaks form where the ratings cluster, while empty bars appear where there are few or no ratings, often due to misaligned bin boundaries and overly fine granularity.

What are the peaks and what are the empty bars?

The peaks in the histogram represent ranges of ratings where many movies share similar scores, indicating clusters at popular ratings. These peaks show which ratings are most common among the movies. In contrast, the empty bars represent bins that contain very few or no movies, indicating that the corresponding rating range is either uncommon or entirely unrepresented. This uneven distribution highlights the fact that movie ratings are not evenly spread across all possible values but are instead concentrated at certain popular points.

What do they tell you about choosing the bin size in histograms?

The presence of pronounced peaks and empty bars suggests that the chosen bin size has a significant impact on how well the histogram represents the data distribution. A bin size that is too small leads to excessive noise and gaps between bins, while a bin size that is too large can oversimplify the data. For discrete data like movie ratings, it seems to be crucial to select a bin size that aligns well with the rating intervals to accurately reflect the clustering of the data and avoid creating misleading patterns in the histogram.

No, I think the take-away is that you should understand how the inappropriate number of bins can mislead you and you should try multiple number of bins to obtain the most accurate picture of the data.
